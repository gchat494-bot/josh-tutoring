The SAT Combination Shortcut

This is one of the most powerful time-saving tricks on the entire SAT. Instead of solving for x and y individually, you can often find a combination like x + y or 2x − y in a single step.

The Key Insight

When the SAT asks for a combination of variables — like x + y, 2x − y, or 3x + 2y — you don't always need to solve for x and y separately. You can often get the answer by simply adding or subtracting the original equations directly.

This can turn a multi-step problem into a one-step problem. Recognize it, use it, and move on.

Side-by-Side Comparison

Slow Way (Solve for x and y individually)

3x + 2y = 20
x − 2y = 4

→ Add: 4x = 24 → x = 6
→ Back-sub: y = 1
→ Then compute: x + y = 7

4 steps total

Fast Way (SAT Shortcut)

3x + 2y = 20
x − 2y = 4

→ Add directly:
(3x + x) + (2y − 2y) = 24
→ 4x = 24... wait, question asks for x + y

Subtract instead: 2x + 4y = 16 → x + 2y = 8... try different combos!

Important: The shortcut only works when the combination you need matches what you get by adding or subtracting the equations. Always check what the problem is actually asking for before choosing your operation.

Worked Example — Classic SAT Shortcut
Example: What is the value of x + y?
3x − 2y = 14
5x + 2y = 34
1
The question asks for x + y. Look at what happens when you add the equations.
2
Add: (3x + 5x) + (−2y + 2y) = 14 + 34 → 8x = 48 → x = 6
3
Back-sub to find y: 3(6) − 2y = 14 → 2y = 4 → y = 2
✓
x + y = 6 + 2 = 8
Example: What is the value of 2x + y? (True one-step shortcut)
3x + y = 15
x − y = 5
1
The question asks for 2x + y (not x and y separately). Try subtracting equation 2 from equation 1.
2
Subtract eq 2 from eq 1: (3x − x) + (y − (−y)) = 15 − 5 → 2x + 2y = 10. Not quite.
3
Try adding: (3x + x) + (y − y) = 20 → 4x = 20 → x = 5. Then y = 0. So 2x + y = 10.
💡
When no single operation gives the combination, just solve normally and compute the combination at the end.

Pattern recognition: On the SAT, if you see a system of two equations and the question asks for something like x + y, x − y, or ax + by — immediately try adding and subtracting the two equations and see if the result matches what's being asked. This appears on almost every SAT!
