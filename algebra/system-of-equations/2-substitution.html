Solving by Substitution

Substitution is the most intuitive method for solving systems. It works by replacing one variable with an expression so you're left with just one equation and one unknown.

When to Use Substitution

Best for: Systems where one equation already has a variable isolated — like y = 3x + 2 or x = 5 − y. If you see this, go straight to substitution without hesitating.

The 4 Steps
1
Isolate one variable in one of the equations. Pick whichever variable is easiest to isolate — usually the one with no coefficient.
2
Substitute that expression into the other equation. Now you have one equation with one variable.
3
Solve for the remaining variable.
4
Back-substitute your answer into either original equation to find the other variable.
Worked Example 1 — Variable Already Isolated
Example 1
y = 2x + 1
3x + y = 16
1
y is already isolated in equation 1: y = 2x + 1
2
Substitute into equation 2: 3x + (2x + 1) = 16
3
Simplify and solve: 5x + 1 = 16 → 5x = 15 → x = 3
4
Back-substitute: y = 2(3) + 1 = 7
✓
Answer: (x, y) = (3, 7)
Worked Example 2 — Must Isolate First
Example 2
x + 2y = 10
3x − y = 5
1
Isolate x in equation 1: x = 10 − 2y
2
Substitute into equation 2: 3(10 − 2y) − y = 5
3
Simplify: 30 − 6y − y = 5 → −7y = −25 → y = 25/7... hmm, messy fractions!
💡
When substitution gives messy fractions, consider switching to Elimination instead (see Lesson 3).

Watch out: Always substitute back into one of the original equations to find the second variable — not into a modified version you created mid-solve. This avoids carrying errors forward.
